Google Summer of Code 2014

Coprocessors in GNU Radio

GNU Radio as a digital signal processing program requires numerous mathematical operations to be executed readily and repeatedly. A faster way to process signals allows projects with high timing constraints such as channel sounding. Past co-processor projects including GPUs, FPGAs, and DSPs do not scale well with new devices. This summer we would like to implement a purely open source three step approach that would improve the state of GNU Radio co-processing for years to come.

The project involved getting intimate with how TI's software handles interprocessor communication as well as modifying GNU Radio and writing a linux kernel driver so that the buffers can be shared among the heterogenous architecture. Going in I knew one summer wasn't enough time to finish so I'm going to put the pieces together during a relatively free semester.

Full details can be found on the GNU Radio wiki

GNU Radio Tutorials Taskforce

Guided Tutorials for Beginners

These tutorials are designed to guide new GNU Radio users to a better understanding of the project: how to work with it, interact with the library, and build your own components and blocks. By the end, you should be comfortable with many of the capabilities of GNU Radio and have some sense for the available tools, functions, and tricks to building your own signal processing project.

Being new to GNU Radio at the time, I created tutorial 2 (GRC) and tutorial 3 (python) as I was most able to relate to other beginners. I went a little overboard and even got parts of my tutorials in the Extras section (E1)!

The full details are available on the GNU Radio wiki

Wireless Club Bug

Teaching PCB Design with Eagle

The goal was to teach students how to use Eagle (available on the school's computers) and raise student interest in wireless. We came up with a bug. Basically you turn it on and put it in a room nearby and listen in on the FM channels. The entire project is $10 and serves as a way to teach students how to use Eagle and the school's surface mount station.

Students liked the simplicity of it and the hands on experience but wished I had written a manual so they can revisit it later when they work on more complex designs. A github wiki page is on the TODO.

A completed project, bill of materials, and library can be found in the wireless repo.

Kinematic Lute

Mechatronic Instrument


The kinematic lute is a project part of the Orpheus and Eurydice: Electromechanical Redux show at the Slought Foundation in Philadelphia. The 3D printed ABS shell hangs from a ceiling using fishing line and makes lights and sounds in response to button presses on a remote.

The project involved messing with ALSA to allow mixing, playing with NeoPixel leds so we can easily control 53 LEDs independently with a single micro, and making a startup routine to run the music playing code. I wrote the code for the AVR's and the BBB, designed the schematics, and wrote it all up on the wiki.

The full details can be found on the github wiki page

APRo

Application-Based Robot

The project goal is to create an application-based robotic platform geared towards the hacker and hobbyist communities that enables the development of robotic applications with multi-purpose hardware. Much like the Arduino catalyzed the microcontroller market and smart phones pioneered mobile applications, the proposed framework is designed to springboard robotic applications.

I am the tech lead responsible for architecting the electronics and software. I have designed circuits in kicad (truly open source hardware) and redesigned a few to make them mass manufacturable. I wrote a recipe for openembedded to build our custom linux distribution and helped develop ideas for mechanical systems and entrepreneurship plans.

This project is still under heavy development at the surveycorps

DarkLamp

World's Most Efficient Lamp


The lamp is powered by a USB cable and uses a light sensor to detect changes in ambient light. The darker it is, the more intense the light hence darklamp. Interesting things happen when you mix light in the dark and vice versa!

I was responsible for creating the schematic and writing the wiki.

The full details can be found on the github wiki page

SwitchBlade

Homebrew RC Car

This is the project that started it all! Sade and I came up with an idea one summer and decided to tackle it on top of our summer internships/research in a way that costs us little money. We used electrical and mechanical components available at school, the school's free 3D printers, and even the school's printed circuit board milling machine the LPKF S62. Due to our lack of experience, the mechanical design limited the electrical design board space, the five wire servo we found laying around for steering had no documentation, and we even completely left out an on/off switch! Of course, we learned from our mistakes and are revamping the project soon.

I was responsible for the schematics, board layout, board manufacturing, and the code.

The code for the old iteration can be found on the github.

NeatoDash

Autonomous Robotic Security System


NeatoDash is a project that hacks the Neato XV-11 to form an Autonomous Lidar Robotic Security System. Although its original function was to be a goalie in a robot soccer tournament, we learned that the serial communication of the Neato is far too slow to process the commands. In a controlled environment it can locate targets and intimidate them with a shiny rubber band gun. If that fails, then it will take them down.

I disassembled the robot, soldered to the serial, and put it back together. I wrote the code for the various functionality and lasercut the addons.

The full details can be found on the github wiki page

ProtoDrive

Increasing Electric Vehicle Efficiency

An experimental platform enabling rapid prototyping and simulation of electric vehicle powertrains. The powertrain is modeled at the small-scale in hardware, making it low-cost and compact enough to fit on a desk. It consists of a physical model of an electric vehicle powertrain coupled to an active dynamometer, making it possible to run the powertrain through its full speed and torque range. The fact that this system has been constructed in hardware allows it to capture intricacies in vehicle operation that may be missed by simulation in software alone.

My contribution involved writing the code for the controls and manufacturing a few boards that we designed.

The details are in the source and webpage
